What creatine actually does — and why dose matters

Creatine works by increasing phosphocreatine stores in muscle tissue, which accelerates ATP regeneration during high-intensity efforts. The practical effects are well established: improved strength output, faster recovery between sets, increased lean muscle mass over time, and emerging evidence of cognitive benefits. None of this is marketing — it's the most replicated finding in the supplement literature.

The effective dose is 3–5g per day, taken consistently. This isn't negotiable. Below 3g, muscle saturation is slow and incomplete. Above 5g, you don't get more benefit — you just waste money. There's no meaningful difference between taking it pre-workout, post-workout, or with breakfast. What matters is daily consistency, not timing.

Loading phases (20g/day for 5–7 days) do accelerate saturation, but they're optional. If you load, split doses across 4–5 servings per day to minimize any GI discomfort. If you skip loading and just take 5g daily from day one, you'll reach the same saturated state within 3–4 weeks.

Monohydrate vs HCL — what the research actually says

Creatine HCL's marketing rests on one real fact: it dissolves more cleanly in water. That's it. That single legitimate property — higher solubility — has been stretched into claims of superior absorption, less bloating, and better performance at lower doses. None of those claims hold up.

The most rigorous head-to-head evidence comes from a 2025 placebo-controlled trial by Bonilla, Kreider, and colleagues, published in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition. Thirty-one elite handball and softball athletes took either 5g of monohydrate, 5g of HCL, or placebo daily for 8 weeks. The result: no statistically significant difference between the two creatine groups on any measure — strength, jump performance, or body composition. The authors concluded that claims of HCL superiority are "unfounded and misleading."

The reason comes down to basic chemistry. Creatine monohydrate already has approximately 99% bioavailability at standard doses. Once HCL reaches stomach acid (pH 1.5–3.5), the hydrochloride group dissociates and both forms yield identical free creatine molecules absorbed through the same intestinal transporters. Higher solubility in water doesn't translate to better absorption in the human body. There is simply no room for improvement over 99%.

The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) position stand — the field's most authoritative consensus document — explicitly identifies the claim that "newer creatine forms are superior to monohydrate" as a myth. It names creatine monohydrate as the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement currently available, period.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to do a loading phase?

No. Loading (20g/day for 5–7 days) gets you to full muscle saturation about 3 weeks faster than just taking 5g daily, but you'll reach the same endpoint either way. If you want results slightly sooner, load — but split the 20g across 4–5 doses to avoid any GI discomfort. If you don't want to bother, just start with 5g daily and be patient.

Does creatine cause kidney damage?

No, in healthy individuals. This myth stems from a misunderstanding of creatinine (a creatine metabolite) being a kidney stress marker. Studies lasting up to 14 years, including doses as high as 30g per day for 5 years, have found no adverse kidney effects in people with healthy kidney function. If you have pre-existing kidney disease, consult a doctor first — but for healthy adults, the safety record is exceptionally clear.

Will creatine make me look "puffy" or cause bloating?

Creatine draws water into muscle cells, which increases intramuscular hydration. This is part of how it works — that water is inside the muscle, not under the skin. Most people notice a slight increase in the scale (1–2kg) in the first week, which is water retained inside muscle tissue, not fat or subcutaneous water retention. The "bloating" reputation comes largely from the old loading phase practice of taking 20g+ in a single dose, which genuinely can cause GI discomfort. At 5g daily, it's rare.

Can I take creatine indefinitely or should I cycle off?

The research supports indefinite daily use with no evidence of harm. Cycling off is a common practice but has no clinical basis — there's no evidence that taking breaks is necessary or beneficial. The muscle creatine stores you've built up will decline within 4–6 weeks of stopping, so cycling off actually costs you the benefit you've built. Unless you have a specific reason to stop, consistent daily use is the evidence-based approach.
